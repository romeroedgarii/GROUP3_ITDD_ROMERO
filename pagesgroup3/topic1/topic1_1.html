DEFINITION & HISTORY OF ERGONOMICS


In today's fast-paced, technology-driven world, more people than ever are spending long hours interacting with computers, gadgets, and digital systems. This digital shift has transformed the modern workspace, particularly in fields like Information Technology (IT), where employees are often seated for extended periods, performing repetitive tasks under mental pressure. As such, the science of ergonomics has become an essential component in designing healthier, more comfortable, and more efficient environments. Ergonomics offers tools and strategies to reduce the physical and mental strain that often results from poorly designed work setups, thereby improving overall well-being and job performance. By recognizing the human body’s needs and limitations, ergonomic design empowers workers to perform better while avoiding injury and burnout. To fully understand its value, it’s important to explore what ergonomics means, how it originated, and how it continues to shape the way we interact with technology and the workplace.



WHAT IS ERGONOMICS?



Ergonomics is the scientific discipline concerned with understanding the interactions among humans and other elements of a system. According to the International Ergonomics Association (IEA), ergonomics “applies theory, principles, data, and methods to design in order to optimize human well-being and overall system performance” (International Ergonomics Association, 2024). In simpler terms, ergonomics is about adapting tools, environments, and processes to fit human capabilities, rather than forcing people to adapt to designs that might harm their health or hinder performance. It draws on knowledge from disciplines such as anatomy, physiology, psychology, engineering, and design to create user-centered solutions for real-world tasks and systems. In the field of Information Technology, where digital interaction is central, ergonomics is especially vital. IT professionals, programmers, data analysts, and many others spend hours typing, clicking, and focusing on screens, often without sufficient movement or support. Ergonomic design in this context includes furniture that promotes good posture, peripherals like split keyboards or vertical mice to reduce joint strain, and software that limits cognitive overload or eye fatigue. These tools are not only meant to prevent injuries but also to promote long-term comfort and sustained productivity throughout the workday.


Good ergonomics goes beyond physical adjustments—it also considers mental and emotional well-being. When workers feel physically comfortable and mentally supported, they are more likely to stay focused, less likely to experience fatigue, and better equipped to manage stress. This holistic approach helps reduce absenteeism, boosts morale, and enhances overall job satisfaction. Ultimately, ergonomics supports the seamless integration of people, technology, and tasks, ensuring that both individual health and system performance thrive in any modern workspace.



THE ORIGINS OF ERGONOMICS



The term "ergonomics" comes from the Greek words ergon (meaning "work") and nomos (meaning "laws") (Dul, J. and Weerdmeester, B., 2008). Although people have always made efforts to design tools and workplaces to suit human use, ergonomics as a formal science emerged during World War II. During this time, engineers and psychologists collaborated to improve the design of aircraft, weapons, and equipment so that they could be operated more safely and efficiently under stressful conditions. This collaboration highlighted the need for a systematic approach to fitting technology to human capabilities and limitations, ultimately giving birth to the modern field of ergonomics. The war emphasized the critical importance of human-centered design, especially when human error could mean life or death. This marked a major shift from seeing people as mere operators of machines to recognizing them as integral parts of complex systems that needed tailored support.


Even before its formal establishment, the basic ideas behind ergonomics can be traced back to ancient civilizations. For instance, archaeological evidence shows that ancient Egyptians and Greeks designed their tools and furniture with an understanding of comfort and functionality. Ancient Roman roads, aqueducts, and military gear also displayed signs of human-centered engineering. In the Renaissance period, artists and inventors like Leonardo da Vinci sketched tools and machinery based on human anatomy and motion. However, it was the technological complexity and industrial demands of the 20th century that demanded a more scientific and organized approach. As machines became faster and more powerful, understanding the limits of human perception, cognition, and movement became essential — thus leading to the emergence of ergonomics as a structured discipline grounded in interdisciplinary research.




EVOLUTION OF ERGONOMICS IN MODERN WORKSPACES



After the war, ergonomics expanded rapidly into civilian industries such as manufacturing, healthcare, and office work. As economies industrialized and job roles became increasingly specialized, the demand for safer and more efficient work environments grew. Ergonomics played a key role in reducing workplace injuries, improving worker satisfaction, and enhancing overall productivity. With the rise of the digital age, the focus shifted significantly toward computer and technology use, introducing new physical and mental challenges. In IT, ergonomic principles began addressing issues like poor posture, repetitive strain injuries (RSIs), eye strain, and mental fatigue caused by prolonged screen exposure. These concerns became especially relevant as more jobs required extended hours of desk-based tasks, highlighting the importance of workstation design and user-centered technology. Today, ergonomics continues to evolve with innovations like adjustable desks, ergonomic keyboards, improved seating designs, and software that promotes healthy work habits through reminders, posture tracking, and screen time regulation. The goal remains the same: to create safer, more comfortable, and more efficient environments that support human performance and long-term health.


Modern workplace ergonomics now also includes designing for remote and hybrid work setups. The COVID-19 pandemic significantly accelerated this shift, with millions of people setting up makeshift workstations at home, often without proper ergonomic guidance. As a result, there is greater emphasis on creating flexible, customizable ergonomic solutions that individuals can easily adapt to their unique spaces—whether it’s a corner of a living room or a fully equipped home office. Employers and designers are now prioritizing compact and affordable ergonomic tools that support healthy posture, movement, and focus regardless of the environment. Furthermore, emerging technologies like virtual reality (VR) and artificial intelligence (AI) are starting to influence ergonomic research, paving the way for even smarter, more personalized approaches to health and productivity in the digital workspace. These advancements allow for real-time data tracking and adaptive systems that adjust to a user’s behavior and needs, bringing ergonomics into a new era of precision and responsiveness.
